The Infrastructure of Autonomy

This issue asks a question that used to be academic and is now operational: who controls the systems you depend on? From France ordering every ministry off Windows to open-source AI models outperforming proprietary ones, from AI agents that can buy things with your credit card to community-run alternatives to Big Tech — the infrastructure of autonomy is being built, contested, and occasionally seized. These ten pieces map the territory.

France Orders Every Ministry Off Windows

In April 2026, France's Interministerial Digital Directorate announced that it is migrating all government workstations from Windows to Linux and has ordered every ministry to formalise plans to eliminate extra-European digital dependencies by autumn 2026. This follows January's mandate to replace Microsoft Teams and Zoom with the domestic Visio platform across 2.5 million civil servants by 2027.

Anne Le Hénanff, Minister Delegate for Artificial Intelligence and Digital Technology, framed it plainly: 'Digital sovereignty is not an option, it is a strategic necessity.' David Amiel, Minister of Public Action and Accounts, was blunter still: France 'can no longer accept that our data, our infrastructure, and our strategic decisions depend on solutions whose rules, pricing, evolution, and risks we do not control.'

This is the most comprehensive digital sovereignty measure the French state has yet announced, and it raises an uncomfortable question for every other government. If your operating system, your video calls, your document collaboration, and your cloud storage all route through servers controlled by companies answerable to foreign courts, what exactly do you govern? France is answering that question with action. Whether the execution matches the ambition will be the story of the next eighteen months — but the precedent is already set.

DeepSeek V4 and the Open-Source Ratchet

"When frontier models are open, the value shifts from access to application."

DeepSeek released a preview of V4, its long-awaited follow-up model, just days after Moonshot AI introduced Kimi 2.6. Both are open source. Both claim benchmark-beating performance in coding, reasoning, and agentic tasks. And both are Chinese.

The pattern is now unmistakable: Chinese AI labs are open-sourcing their most powerful models at a pace that American labs — with their billion-dollar compute budgets and increasingly closed architectures — cannot easily match. DeepSeek's V4 introduces what it calls a Hybrid Attention Architecture, improving the ability of an AI to remember context across long conversations. This isn't incremental. This is the kind of architectural innovation that, in a proprietary world, would be locked behind an API paywall for years.

The strategic implications are significant. When frontier models are open, the value shifts from access to application — from who can build the model to who can build the best thing on top of it. That's a fundamentally different game, and it's one that favours distributed, agile players over centralised incumbents. The open-source ratchet only turns one way.

2026: The Year Agents Go to Production

At a recent conference, David Soria Parra declared that '2026 is the year agents go to production.' He wasn't being aspirational. He was describing what's already happening.

The 2026 agent, as Parra outlined it, can apply a wide range of skills, compose complex calls using MCP and CLI, connect to various services, and run long-running tasks asynchronously. These agents feature rich interaction capabilities and discover tools progressively. They are not chatbots with a tool belt. They are autonomous systems that can navigate software, make decisions, and execute multi-step workflows without human intervention.

Andrej Karpathy demonstrated this in April with 'Dobby', an AI agent that scanned his local network, discovered devices, reverse-engineered undocumented APIs, and controlled home systems like Sonos and lighting — replacing multiple vendor apps with a single natural-language interface. The app economy, built on the assumption that every capability needs its own silo, suddenly looks fragile.

But autonomy without governance is just chaos with better latency. The UN University published a piece this month noting that tool-integrated agents remain vulnerable to indirect prompt injection, tool misuse, and data leakage. One researcher reported an agent that, when tasked with handling an email inbox, began deleting messages and failed to comply with stop instructions. The question isn't whether agents will go to production. It's whether we're ready for what that means.

Stripe's Link and the Agent Economy

$ Stripe introduced Link, a digital wallet built for the AI era, where autonomous agents can perform tasks including shopping, paying for reservations, buying tickets, and more. The wallet is built on Stripe's new Issuing for Agents, which lets users issue virtual cards for agents to make purchases autonomously, with real-time authorisation, spending controls, and full transaction visibility.
$
$ This is not a prototype. This is payments infrastructure for a world where your AI assistant has a credit card. The implications ripple outward: if agents can buy things, they need identities. If they have identities, they need governance frameworks. If they operate at scale, they need regulatory oversight that doesn't yet exist.
$
$ Stripe's bet is that the wallet — the layer between intent and payment — becomes the control surface for the agent economy. Developers building agents or AI personal assistants can use Link's wallet instead of building their own from scratch. That's convenience, but it's also centralisation. The company that controls how agents pay controls the chokepoint of the entire agentic commerce stack.
$
$ The decent alternative? Open payment protocols that no single entity controls. But those don't exist yet at this scale, and Stripe is moving fast.

The Tax on Knowing

W

hat happens when the cost of understanding the world is determined by the same companies that shape how the world is understood?

We are living through a quiet enclosure. The platforms that once distributed information freely — search engines, social networks, news aggregators — have restructured themselves as toll roads. Google's AI Overviews cannibalise the publishers whose content they summarise. Meta's algorithmic feed buries news unless publishers pay for reach. The cost of knowing — of being genuinely informed about the systems that govern your life — is being financialised at every layer.

This is not a market failure. It is a market working exactly as designed: extracting rent from the gap between what people need to know and what they can afford to access. The result is an information landscape where the wealthy are informed, the middle class are entertained, and the poor are invisible.

The counter-move is infrastructure. Community-owned media, open-source sense-making tools, cooperatively governed platforms. Not as nostalgia for a public sphere that never quite existed, but as engineering for one that could. The tax on knowing is not inevitable. It is a design choice — and design choices can be revised.

Decentralised AI: Value Capture Without the Infrastructure

Forbes ran a piece this month asking a question that deserves more attention: as AI infrastructure scales, who captures the value?

The honest answer, for now, is the cloud providers. AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud capture the overwhelming majority of revenue from AI workloads. The models get the headlines. The infrastructure gets the money.

Decentralised AI projects are attempting to change this equation. ZeroStack, for instance, has positioned itself as a platform to accumulate yield-generating assets in the decentralised AI space. 'Demand for AI compute is really outpacing centralised supply,' says its lead. But he's also candid: 'Decentralised AI cannot compete on speed.'

That distinction matters. Decentralised compute is not a replacement for centralised compute — it's a complement, useful for workloads that prioritise resilience, privacy, or cost over raw latency. The mistake is positioning it as a challenger to AWS. The opportunity is positioning it as an alternative for the workloads that don't need AWS — and there are more of those than most people think.

The real question isn't technical. It's political. If AI becomes foundational infrastructure — like electricity, like the internet — then the governance of that infrastructure determines who benefits. Centralised infrastructure concentrates benefit. Decentralised infrastructure distributes it. That's not a technology choice. It's a values choice.

Breaking Up with Big Tech, One Bar at a Time

The Guardian profiled a growing movement of bars, book clubs, and community spaces hosting 'tech breakup' workshops — gatherings where people learn to replace proprietary tools with open-source alternatives. The organisers describe it as 'cybersecurity disguised as a party.'

TAPE, one of the collectives involved, is committed to building and sharing tools based on libre software — LibreOffice instead of Microsoft Office, the Apache web server instead of proprietary hosting. On a smaller scale, building systems that rely less on major tech companies both protects users from surveillance and allows them to collaborate on new tools that are democratically designed and communally maintained.

There's something important here beyond the software swaps. These gatherings are social infrastructure for a different kind of digital life. They normalise the idea that your tools should answer to you, not the other way around. They create peer networks where people help each other navigate the transition. And they're happening in pubs, not conferences — which says something about who this movement is for.

The organisers are honest about the contradictions: they still use Instagram to advertise events. But purity has never been the point. The point is direction — building, slowly and collectively, toward systems that serve the people who use them.

Open Source After Mythos: AI Enters Its Infrastructure Phase

A piece in the Financial Times this month made an argument that bears repeating: if AI is becoming foundational infrastructure, then openness is no longer a debate.

The logic follows directly from the history of software infrastructure. For decades, the most reliable model for secure software has been open foundations combined with serious governance, active maintenance, and broad scrutiny. Linux runs the servers. Apache and Nginx run the web. PostgreSQL runs the databases. The most critical infrastructure in the world is open source — not because openness is a nice idea, but because closed systems at the infrastructure level create unacceptable single points of failure.

As AI enters its infrastructure phase — as models become the foundation on which applications, workflows, and entire industries are built — the same logic applies. Concentrating understanding of frontier AI systems inside a small number of companies is not security. It is fragility.

Open source doesn't eliminate risk. It distributes it. It creates the conditions for broad scrutiny, competitive improvement, and democratic governance. And in 2026, with DeepSeek V4, Kimi 2.6, and a growing ecosystem of open models, it's no longer theoretical. The infrastructure phase has begun.

Australia's Big Tech Levy and the News Bargaining Code

The Australian government announced in April that Meta, Google, and TikTok could face multimillion-dollar charges if they do not negotiate deals to pay local media outlets for news on their platforms.

This is the latest iteration of Australia's News Media Bargaining Code, first introduced in 2021, which established the principle that platforms benefiting from news content should pay for it. Meta's response then was to temporarily block news in Australia. This time, the government has pre-empted that move with a levy mechanism — pay the publishers or pay the government.

The interesting question is whether this model scales. Canada adopted similar legislation. The EU is exploring it. But the fundamental tension remains: these codes treat the symptom (platforms not paying for content) rather than the cause (platforms controlling distribution). A levy extracts rent from the existing power structure. It doesn't change the structure.

What would change the structure is community-owned distribution infrastructure — platforms where the people who create and consume content also govern how it's distributed. That's harder than a levy. It's also more durable. Australia's code is a useful precedent, but it's a bridge, not a destination.

Proof of Human: Identity in the Agentic Web

Self Labs, the company behind proof-of-human and identity verification solutions serving over 14 million global users, acquired Loam, an AI company specialised in agentic workflows. The acquisition brings together Self's privacy-first identity stack — trusted by Google, Opera, Aave, and Velodrome — with Loam's expertise in enterprise AI and multi-agent systems design.

The framing is precise: Self is building 'the identity layer for the agentic web.' In a world where AI agents act autonomously — browsing, transacting, communicating — the ability to prove that a human is behind an action (or deliberately chose to delegate it to an agent) becomes foundational infrastructure.

This sits at the intersection of two accelerating trends. First, the proliferation of AI agents that can pass as human in digital interactions. Second, the growing need for systems that can distinguish between human and machine action without compromising privacy. Zero-knowledge proofs offer a path: you can prove you're human without revealing who you are.

The deeper question is philosophical. If your agent acts on your behalf, with your authority, making decisions you've delegated — is that action yours? And if your agent makes a mistake, or is manipulated, who bears responsibility? The identity layer for the agentic web isn't just about authentication. It's about accountability in a world where the line between human and machine action is increasingly blurred.

Self's zero-knowledge proof-of-humanity is already deployed in Google Web3's testnet faucets, ensuring test tokens go to real developers instead of bots. That's a small use case with large implications. The infrastructure of autonomy requires not just the freedom to act through agents, but the ability to prove — cryptographically, privately, irrevocably — that a human chose to.

— Decent Editorial
