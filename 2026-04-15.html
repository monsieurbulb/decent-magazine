The Case for Decentralised Infrastructure

We are entering an era where the infrastructure that underpins our digital lives is being rebuilt from the ground up. The old model — centralised servers, corporate gatekeepers, rented access — is giving way to something more resilient. Decentralised networks don't just distribute data, they distribute power. The question is no longer whether this shift will happen, but who gets to shape it.

The challenge is making these systems as intuitive as the ones they replace. Technical elegance means nothing if it can't be used by the people who need it most.

— Richard Wermerling

Soil: Growing a New Kind of Framework

Soil is a framework for building blockchain runtimes — a fork and evolution of Substrate that strips away corporate governance in favour of community-driven development. It asks a simple question: what happens when you treat infrastructure as a commons?

The answer, it turns out, is a lot of weeding. Open-source maintenance is unglamorous work, but it's the foundation everything else grows from. Every merged PR, every resolved issue, every documentation improvement is an act of cultivation.

— The Soil Collective
github.com/nicknick/soil-framework

Why Kabocha Still Matters

Kabocha was born from a conviction that parachains should serve communities, not venture capitalists. Three years on, that conviction hasn't wavered. The parachain runs. The collator syncs. The treasury funds public goods.

In a landscape littered with abandoned chains and empty promises, simply continuing to exist is a radical act. Kabocha doesn't need to be the biggest network — it needs to be the most honest one.

— Parity Dissident

On Running Your Own Server

$ I started running my own server because I was tired of paying rent on my own data.
$ The first month was hell. DNS propagation, SSL certificates, failed systemd services at 3am.
$ The second month was better. I automated the backups. Set up monitoring. Wrote scripts.
$ By the third month, I understood something important: the server wasn't just a machine.
$ It was a statement. My files. My rules. My responsibility.
$ Not everyone needs to do this. But everyone should be able to.

No. 5

The Treasury Problem

O

n-chain treasuries were supposed to solve the funding problem for public goods. In practice, they've created a new class of bureaucracy — one that's transparent but no less political. Proposals are gamed. Voters are apathetic. The biggest grants go to the loudest voices, not the most important work.

This isn't a failure of technology. It's a failure of design. We built treasuries that mirror the institutions they were meant to replace. The next generation needs to be radically different: smaller, faster, more opinionated about what matters.

— Dr. Eleanor Voss

73%

Seventy-three percent of open-source maintainers report burnout. The software that runs the world is held together by exhausted volunteers. This is not a technical problem — it's a social one. Until we build sustainable funding models for the people who maintain our digital commons, we're building on sand.

— Decent Research

Two Visions of the Web

On one side: platforms. Algorithmically curated, attention-optimised, engagement-driven. Your feed is a product. Your data is inventory. Every interaction is measured, monetised, and fed back into the machine.

On the other side: protocols. Open, interoperable, user-controlled. Your identity is portable. Your data is yours. Every interaction is a choice you make with full information.

The first vision won the last decade. The second might win the next. But only if we build it.

— Maya Chen
www.w3.org/TR/did-core/

Notes from the Edge of the Network

I've been running a mesh node in south London for six months. My neighbours think I'm slightly mad. Maybe they're right. The range is terrible. The throughput is worse. But when the internet went down for three hours last Tuesday — BT outage, affecting half the borough — my little node kept working. Four of us stayed connected. We shared files, sent messages, even streamed a podcast.

It wasn't much. But it was ours. And in that moment, 'decentralised' stopped being an abstraction and became a lived experience.

— Signal/Noise

The Compost Manifesto

We propose a new metaphor for digital infrastructure: not the cloud, but the compost heap. Clouds are distant, ethereal, someone else's problem. Compost is local, biological, transformative. It takes what's discarded and makes it fertile. It requires tending. It smells. It works.

Our networks should be more like compost: messy, organic, alive. Let the old platforms decompose into something useful. Feed the soil. Grow something new.

— Humus DAO
humusdao.earth
